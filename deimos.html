Deimos: The Lesser Moon

Deimos Overview

Deimos, the smaller and outermost moon of Mars, was discovered by Asaph Hall in 1877. It has a diameter of about 12 kilometers and a smoother, less cratered appearance compared to Phobos, thanks to a thicker layer of regolith that fills in impacts. Orbiting at around 23,460 kilometers from Mars, Deimos takes about 30 hours to complete one revolution, appearing as a slow-moving star from the Martian surface.

Like Phobos, Deimos is thought to be a captured asteroid, with a composition rich in carbon and possibly water ice. Its low gravity and escape velocity make it an intriguing target for future mining or as a base for Mars missions, as launching from it requires minimal energy. Spectral analysis shows similarities to C-type asteroids, supporting the capture hypothesis.

Deimos experiences minimal tidal effects and is expected to remain stable in orbit for billions of years. As of 2025, it has been imaged by various Mars orbiters, but no dedicated missions have landed there yet. Upcoming explorations, including potential human outposts, could leverage Deimos for resources, reducing the costs of deeper space travel.
