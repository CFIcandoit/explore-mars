Olympus Mons: The Largest Mountain

Olympus Mons Overview

Olympus Mons is the tallest volcano in the solar system, located on Mars' Tharsis region, rising about 22 kilometers above the surrounding plainsâ€”nearly three times the height of Mount Everest. This shield volcano spans 600 kilometers at its base, formed by repeated lava flows over billions of years with low viscosity basalt that spread widely. Its gentle slopes, averaging 5 degrees, result from the planet's lower gravity allowing for such massive build-up without collapse.

The volcano's caldera, a complex of overlapping craters at the summit, measures up to 85 kilometers wide and 3 kilometers deep, indicating multiple eruption phases. Last active around 25 million years ago, Olympus Mons shows no current signs of volcanism, but Mars' geological youth suggests potential future activity. Cliff-like scarps around its base, up to 8 kilometers high, formed from landslides or tectonic shifts.

As of 2025, orbital imagery from missions like Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter has revealed detailed lava tubes and flow patterns, aiding studies of Martian geology. Olympus Mons exemplifies the planet's extreme volcanism, influenced by a lack of plate tectonics, concentrating eruptions in hotspots. It remains a key site for understanding planetary formation and a potential destination for future robotic or human explorers.
