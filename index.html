Mars: The Red Planet

Mars Overview

Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, is often called the "Red Planet" due to its iron-rich soil that gives it a reddish hue. It has a thin atmosphere primarily composed of carbon dioxide, with surface temperatures ranging from about -140°C to 20°C. Mars is roughly half the size of Earth, with a diameter of approximately 6,779 kilometers, and it completes an orbit around the Sun every 687 Earth days. The planet features diverse geological formations, including vast plains, deep canyons, and towering volcanoes, making it a prime target for scientific exploration.

Human interest in Mars has surged with missions like NASA's Perseverance rover, which landed in 2021 and continues to search for signs of ancient microbial life as of 2025. Evidence suggests that Mars once had liquid water on its surface, with features like dried riverbeds and polar ice caps hinting at a wetter past. Current atmospheric pressure is too low for liquid water to exist stably, but subsurface reservoirs or seasonal flows remain possibilities under investigation.

Future plans for Mars include crewed missions, with organizations like SpaceX aiming for human settlement. Challenges include radiation exposure, dust storms that can last months, and the need for sustainable habitats. As of 2025, international collaborations are advancing technologies for in-situ resource utilization, such as extracting oxygen from the atmosphere, to support long-term exploration.

Moons of Mars

Phobos

Mars’ larger, innermost moon, a 22-kilometer-wide, cratered, potato-shaped body, orbits every 7.65 hours and appears as a bright, star-like object from the surface.

Deimos

Mars’ smaller, outer moon, about 12 kilometers in diameter, orbits every 30 hours, appearing as a faint, star-like point in the Martian sky.

Features of Mars

Olympus Mons

The solar system’s tallest shield volcano, standing 22 kilometers high on Mars, formed by extensive lava flows and inactive for roughly 25 million years.

Valles Marineris

A massive Martian canyon system, over 4,000 kilometers long, shaped by tectonic rifting and erosion, with evidence of ancient water flows.
